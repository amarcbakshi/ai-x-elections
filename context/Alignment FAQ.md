# AI x Elections — Alignment FAQ

*Source of truth for team members, funders, and partners. All other project documents should be consistent with this one.*

---

## What is AI x Elections?

AI x Elections is a bi-weekly professional newsletter for county and state election officials covering the intersection of artificial intelligence and election administration. It covers threats, tools, vendor developments, field intelligence, and best practices — with "AI x elections" as the organizing editorial frame. It is a trade publication, not an advocacy newsletter.

---

## Who is the audience?

**Primary:** County and state election officials — understaffed, vendor-skeptical, protective of their professional credibility, time-scarce. They read newsletters to stay current, not to be sold to or recruited.

**Secondary:** Journalists covering election administration, civic tech practitioners, policy researchers who track election law and infrastructure.

The publication is not written for the general public. It is not written for political activists or partisans. It is written for the people who actually administer elections.

---

## What problem does it solve?

Election officials have no dedicated professional publication. They currently navigate the AI threat landscape through: Signal threads, county-to-county phone calls, occasional convenings, and whatever they can piece together from general-interest reporting that rarely provides the operational depth they need. There is no clearinghouse for "what do I do when I receive a 1,000-voter challenge list" or "how do I evaluate an AI vendor pitching me tabulation software." AI x Elections fills that gap with the voice and depth of a serious trade publication.

---

## What is the editorial frame, and why "AI x elections"?

"AI x elections" is the organizing lens, not a marketing hook. Every major threat to election integrity in 2026 has an AI dimension, and using AI as the frame gives the publication license to cover anything election-relevant without triggering the partisanship filters that cause officials to tune out advocacy-coded sources. It is a deliberately non-partisan frame. It is also genuinely novel — no existing publication covers this intersection for practitioners.

---

## What is the distribution strategy?

Gary Smith (civitech.io / BallotReady) has direct email access to election officials nationwide and has expressed interest in co-branding or white-labeling the launch. The newsletter launches under a BallotReady partnership or white-label arrangement, inheriting reach and credibility on day one. Independent newsletter growth is a secondary channel. This is a solved distribution problem.

---

## What is the relationship to Election Mayhem?

Election Mayhem is a general-audience publication covering the full scope of election disruption. AI x Elections is a practitioner-facing publication serving the professionals who have to respond to that disruption. They cover overlapping subject matter from different perspectives. They are distinct products. AI x Elections is not a rebrand of Election Mayhem.

---

## What is the business model?

The publication launches as a mission-driven, free-to-subscribe newsletter. Potential revenue pathways (not a near-term priority): sponsored research or tool guides, practitioner community access, institutional licensing for training programs. The near-term goal is reach and trust, not revenue.

---

## How does this connect to Portfolio's other projects?

The newsletter is also a **demand aggregator**: when officials respond to coverage of a shared problem (e.g., the OCR scanner gap, the absence of a polling place consolidation tool, fragmented voter challenge workflows), the publication has already built the audience into which a solution can be deployed. Media is top-of-funnel; tools are the follow-through. This connects AI x Elections to Portfolio's broader infrastructure-building orientation.

---

## What does the editorial calendar look like?

Fourteen bi-weekly issues, June 2 through November 10, 2026. Editorial phases: Launch (June), Summer threats (July–August), Fall ramp-up (September), October crunch, Election & aftermath (November). Key anchored stories include the Georgia QR code tabulation crisis (June 30 issue, day before the July 1 deadline), the Eagle AI voter challenge ecosystem, polling place consolidation analysis, and pre-election security briefings.

---

## What does success look like in 2026?

- 3,000+ subscribers by issue 5, with meaningful open rates (>40%)
- At least one story that generates coverage in a major outlet or is cited by an official body
- At least one "demand aggregation" cycle completed: story surfaces a problem → tool is built → deployed to newsletter audience
- The publication is cited by election officials as a resource they use and trust
- A credible zero identified and running the publication by issue 4

---

## Who is the zero?

Not yet identified. Target profile: a writer or editor with election law or election administration familiarity, comfort in a practitioner-to-practitioner voice, and capacity to publish on a bi-weekly cadence with AI assistance. Former election reporter, election law scholar, or civic tech practitioner are all plausible backgrounds. Must be someone election officials will take seriously.
