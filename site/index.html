Threat Watch — Lead

The FOIA Flood Is Here

Election offices in at least eleven states are reporting surges in public records requests with a shared signature: near-identical formatting, inhuman volume, and timing calibrated to hit before administrative deadlines. The goal is rarely the records.

A county clerk in a competitive Ohio district received 340 public records requests in a single week last October, up from a typical monthly volume of eight to twelve. Two staff members handle public records in that office. For ten days, they handled almost nothing else. Ballots for a primary were scheduled for mailing the following week. They were late.

The pattern has been documented in Ohio, Wisconsin, Arizona, Georgia, Pennsylvania, North Carolina, and Nevada — states sharing two characteristics: competitive federal races and active election denial organizing networks. It has an informal name inside CISA: AI-assisted records flooding. The objective is not the records. It is staff time.

Legally, the landscape offers little relief. Public records laws in most states do not permit offices to discriminate between human and automated requesters. The volume itself is not illegal. Some attorneys are exploring whether coordinated flooding constitutes obstruction, but no case has advanced past preliminary research.

What to do: Document every anomalous request — volume, formatting patterns, requester information. CISA's election security team wants this data. Three states are drafting legislation allowing offices to charge processing fees for AI-generated volume. Model language is in the Toolkit section below.

Audio Synthesis

Synthetic Audio Impersonating Local Officials

Unlike deepfake video, synthetic audio is cheap, fast, and effective enough to fool most voters.

A convincing approximation of a county clerk's voice can be generated in under an hour using freely available tools and three minutes of public audio from a county commission meeting posted to YouTube. The resulting clip is indistinguishable from authentic audio to most listeners and, in six documented cases over the past eight months, was widely shared before any official correction reached the same audience.

What distinguishes these incidents from generic disinformation: the content is hyper-local and operationally specific. Wrong dates. Wrong polling places. Wrong deadlines. Not national messaging — targeted disruption of a specific jurisdiction's voters.

Suppression Operations

AI-Generated Polling Place Notices Are Spreading

Geographically specific content claiming polling places have closed or moved is the most common AI incident election offices are reporting.

Most originate outside the target county. Many are specific enough — referencing actual street addresses, actual precinct names — to be convincing to voters who don't verify. The production cost is negligible. A flyer, a text blast, a Nextdoor post: each can be generated and distributed in an afternoon.

Three counties have now developed rapid-response protocols. The common elements: a pre-established relationship with local TV assignment desks, a templated correction post ready to deploy, and a direct line to their state CISA contact. The full protocol is documented in Field Reports.

What Signature Verification AI Actually Does

Fourteen companies are marketing AI-powered signature verification for mail ballot processing. We asked all fourteen the same three questions. Most declined to answer.

The questions were not adversarial. We asked: what dataset was your model trained on; is that dataset certified or independently audited; and what is your published false rejection rate broken down by demographic group?

Six companies did not respond after two contact attempts. Four responded with marketing materials. Three provided partial technical documentation. One provided full specifications including third-party audit results. That company's product, it turned out, was trained predominantly on commercial check-processing data — a dataset that does not include election ballot signatures and skews toward populations that write checks frequently.

The EAC certification gap: AI tools that touch vote-adjacent processes — signature matching, mail ballot sorting, voter roll deduplication — are not currently subject to EAC certification requirements. Those requirements apply to voting systems as defined under HAVA, and most vendors have structured their products to fall outside that definition. This is not necessarily intentional evasion; the regulatory framework predates AI-powered election tools. But the gap is real and documented.

Incident Account

When the Video Is Fake and the Phones Start Ringing

A mid-sized Georgia county's first AI impersonation incident: forty-seven calls before 10am, a synthetic video of the election director, and no communications plan.

On a Tuesday morning in March, a video circulating on Facebook and Nextdoor showed what appeared to be the county election director announcing that the voter registration deadline had been moved forward by two weeks. The video was synthetic — the voice a convincing approximation built from three YouTube recordings of county commission meetings, the background a stock image of a county government building.

By 9am, the Clerk's office had received forty-seven calls. By noon, a local TV station had called. By 3pm, the Secretary of State's office was involved. The county had no communications plan, no media contact on retainer, and a website that had not been updated since 2022.

"We have two people," the clerk told us. "One was on the phone all day. The other was trying to figure out who to call." CISA was not on the short list of people she thought to call. It is now.

What she'd do differently: Pre-establish a relationship with the local TV assignment desk before any incident. Have a one-page template for corrections ready to deploy. Know your CISA regional contact's cell number. Have a statement that says "we are aware of the content, it is not from this office, here is how to verify official information from us" drafted before you ever need it.

Federal Guidance

CISA's Election AI Guidance: A Close Reading

CISA published guidance on AI threats to election infrastructure in December 2025. Four pages are directly relevant to election administration. Here is what those pages actually say.

The document commits CISA to briefing state election officials on specific AI threat indicators, providing incident response support for AI-related incidents, and sharing threat intelligence with SoS offices through established channels. It does not create new reporting requirements for election offices, does not establish standards for AI vendor procurement, and does not address AI tools sold to campaigns — which fall outside CISA's jurisdiction.

What remains entirely unaddressed: AI-assisted public records flooding, synthetic media impersonating local (as opposed to statewide) officials, and the certification gap for AI tools in vote-adjacent processes.

Incident Account — Anonymous

"We Had Eight Days and No Playbook"

The election director of a 180,000-voter Mountain West county describes her office's first AI incident. She asked that her county not be named.

"The first call came from a poll worker, not a voter. She'd seen a video on Facebook — someone who looked and sounded like me, announcing that we were moving to a new ballot drop-off location because of a security situation at the courthouse. There was no security situation. There was no new drop-off location. And I had never made a video like that."

The video had been live for eleven hours before the election director's office learned of it. It had been shared 2,300 times. The office's Facebook page had 400 followers. "They had a bigger audience than we did."

Over the following eight days, the office coordinated with the SoS communications team, issued three formal corrections, briefed the county commissioners, and worked with Facebook to have the video removed — a process that took five days and required documentation of the synthetic origin the office was not equipped to provide on its own. CISA assisted with the technical analysis. That relationship, she said, had been established six months earlier at an EAC training. "If we hadn't done that training, I wouldn't have known who to call."
