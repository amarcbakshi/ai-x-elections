Issue No. 01 — July 1, 2025 — Overview

The Vacuum: Why Nobody Is Covering the Story of the Decade

Hundreds of outlets cover artificial intelligence. Dozens cover elections. The intersection — where the technology is actively reshaping democratic outcomes — is a dead zone.

In the eighteen months following the release of GPT-4, journalists produced thousands of words about artificial intelligence's potential to disrupt democratic institutions. Almost none of it was reported. It was speculated, extrapolated, and occasionally hallucinated — by the machines themselves, describing their own dangers with the credulous authority of a press release.

The beat that should exist looks like this: a reporter who understands how large language models work talks to election administrators who are actually confronted with AI-generated disinformation. Who has done that story? Across every major American outlet, you can count the instances on one hand.

The failure is structural. Political desks don't have technical fluency. Technology desks don't have relationships with county clerks in Maricopa or Fulton or Dane County. The result is a coverage pattern in which AI and elections are treated as adjacent phenomena — occasionally colliding in a dramatic incident, then retreating to their separate corners of the internet.

AI × Elections was founded on the premise that this is the most important unreported beat in American journalism. Not because artificial intelligence will necessarily "steal" an election in the cinematic sense — but because the technology is already reshaping how campaigns target voters, how officials verify registrations, how disinformation spreads, and how regulators attempt to respond. All of this is happening in the open. None of it is being systematically documented.

This publication is an attempt to build that institutional record. Bi-weekly dispatches. Reported, not speculated. Written for the practitioners — election officials, campaign technologists, policy staff, researchers — who will be asked to make decisions about this intersection whether or not anyone is covering it.

We begin with the vacuum, because naming it is the first act of journalism. The void does not fill itself.

Issue No. 02 — Jul 15, 2025

Deep Fakes on the Ballot

Synthetic media ran in the 2024 cycle. Some was caught. Most wasn't. A post-mortem on what happened and what wasn't reported.

In the final weeks of the 2024 cycle, a robocall impersonating a presidential candidate told Democratic voters in New Hampshire that voting on primary day would "save your vote" for the November election. The audio was synthesized. The call reached tens of thousands of voters.

The incident was covered as a news story. It was not covered as a symptom. What the New Hampshire incident revealed was an infrastructure — for generating, distributing, and attributing synthetic political content — that had matured without systematic documentation. This issue maps that infrastructure.

Issue No. 03 — Aug 1, 2025

The Micro-Targeting Machine

How campaigns are using large language models to personalize political messaging at a scale that was impossible two years ago.

In the 2022 cycle, a campaign with a sophisticated digital operation might produce several hundred variations of a fundraising email. In 2024, LLM-assisted tools allowed campaigns to generate personalized outreach at the individual voter level — matching message, tone, and issue salience to each recipient's profile.

The technology is not secret. The vendors are registered. The capability is documented in marketing materials. What is absent is any systematic accounting of who deployed it, at what scale, targeting which voters — and whether existing disclosure frameworks cover it at all.

Issue No. 04 — Aug 15, 2025

Who's Regulating AI in Elections?

Federal, state, and international frameworks. What has force of law, what is proposal, and what is guidance with no enforcement mechanism.

As of publication, twenty-three states have enacted legislation that mentions artificial intelligence in the context of elections. Three of those laws contain meaningful enforcement provisions. The remaining twenty contain definitions, study requirements, and pilot program authorizations that have produced no documented enforcement actions.

At the federal level, the FEC issued an advance notice of proposed rulemaking in August 2023. As of this issue, it has not finalized any rule. The FTC has taken one enforcement action related to AI-generated political content. This is the regulatory landscape. This issue maps it without euphemism.
